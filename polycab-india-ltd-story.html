A Family That Lost Everything — Twice

In 1947, when the subcontinent was cleaved apart along religious lines, Thakurdas Jaisinghani was among the millions of Sindhi Hindus who crossed the border into India carrying little more than the clothes on their backs. He made his way to Bombay — the city of reinvention — and by 1964, had built something modest but real: a small electrical hardware shop tucked inside Lohar Chawl, the dense, chaotic market in South Mumbai where the city's traders have bought and sold metals and tools for over a century.

He had four children. The oldest, Inder, was learning the ropes of the shop — the suppliers, the margins, the customers who haggled hard and paid harder. Then in 1968, Thakurdas died. Inder was fifteen years old. There was no savings cushion. No family wealth from before Partition. No one to take over except a boy who should have been in school.

Inder did not go back to school. He went to work.

The Long Years in the Trenches

The 1970s were unglamorous. Inder and his brothers — Ajay, Bharat, and Ramesh — ran the shop, built relationships with electricians and contractors, and slowly began to understand that the real money was not in selling wires but in making them. The traders who came to Lohar Chawl to buy were middlemen. The manufacturers were the ones who set the terms.

The insight was simple, but acting on it was not. Manufacturing required land, machinery, and capital — things the Jaisinghani brothers did not have. But in 1975, through sheer persistence and a willingness to bet everything, they acquired a plot in MIDC Andheri and built their first cable manufacturing plant. It was small, undercapitalised, and surrounded by far larger established players. But it was theirs.

The early competition was fierce. In the wires and cables industry of 1970s India, the established names — Finolex, Havells, KEI — had distribution networks built over decades. A small manufacturer from Andheri had no obvious reason to win. What the Jaisindhanis had was something harder to quantify: obsession with quality and a stubborn refusal to cut corners even when margins were thin.

The Secret Weapon: Make It Yourself

What separated Polycab from the traders and distributors who dominated the market was vertical integration. While competitors outsourced manufacturing and focused on distribution, the Jaisinghani family kept expanding their manufacturing footprint — plant after plant, SKU after SKU. By the 2000s, Polycab had one of the broadest product portfolios in Indian electricals: over 10,600 SKUs ranging from the thinnest household wire to high-voltage cables for industrial and infrastructure projects.

Vertical integration gave Polycab something priceless: cost control. When copper prices moved — and in the wire industry, they always move — a manufacturer who owned the process could absorb or pass through those changes faster than a trading company. When customers wanted customisation, Polycab could deliver it without waiting on a supplier. The manufacturing moat became the competitive moat.

The IPO That Nobody Saw Coming

In April 2019, Polycab India went public. For most of India's investing class, the name was unfamiliar. Wires and cables were not the stuff of glamorous equity research notes or cocktail party conversations. But analysts who dug into the numbers found something extraordinary: a company with ₹8,000 Cr-plus revenue, consistent profitability, a dominant market share, and a management that had been at it for over five decades without ever needing venture capital or private equity.

The IPO was subscribed 51.77 times. Qualified institutional buyers oversubscribed their portion by 124 times. On listing day, Polycab opened at a significant premium and has not looked back since. It was the market's way of acknowledging something the industry had known for years: this was not a new story. This was a story that had been quietly compounding for generations.

What a ₹22,000 Crore Company Actually Looks Like

Today, Polycab India is not simply India's largest wires and cables company. It is something more interesting: a family-built institution that spans the entire electrical value chain. From the copper rods that arrive at the factory gate to the finished fan or switch on a retail shelf, Polycab has inserted itself into every stage. The company's FMEG division — launched in 2014 — now sells fans, lights, switches, and solar products through 4,000-plus towns and a distributor network that most pure-play FMCG companies would envy.

The company employs tens of thousands of people across manufacturing plants in Halol, Daman, Nashik, Haridwar, and more. Its export division ships Extra High Voltage cables to over 35 countries. Its next growth frontier — data centre cables and green energy infrastructure — is already being built.

None of this was inevitable. None of it was funded by institutional capital chasing a hot sector. It was built, decade by decade, by a family who had seen what it meant to lose everything and decided they would never be caught unprepared again.

Why This Matters Now

India is in the middle of the largest infrastructure buildout in its history. Power transmission, smart cities, industrial corridors, data centres, rooftop solar, EV charging networks — all of it needs wires and cables. Polycab, with its dominant market share, its manufacturing scale, and its decades of trust with contractors and utilities, is positioned at the centre of this transformation. Revenue crossed ₹22,000 Cr in FY25, growing 24% year-on-year. The management has set the next target — and based on how Polycab has always operated, they will probably hit it early.

But beyond the numbers, this is a story about something older than quarterly earnings. It is about what happens when a family loses everything — not once but twice, across Partition and bereavement — and decides that the only response is to build something that cannot be taken away. Something that employs thousands, electrifies millions of homes, and compounds across generations.

Inder Jaisinghani dropped out of school at fifteen. He is now worth over six billion dollars. Not because of luck or timing, but because he chose to make things rather than just sell them, and he never stopped.
