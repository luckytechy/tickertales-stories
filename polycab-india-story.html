A Shop That Was Never Supposed to Survive

In 1964, Thakurdas Jaisinghani walked into a small shop space in Mumbai's Lohar Chawl — the city's oldest electrical goods market, a narrow lane of tin-roofed stalls where electricians, contractors, and hawkers jostle shoulder to shoulder. He had nothing grand in mind. His family had fled Pakistan during the Partition of 1947, and like thousands of Sindhi families who rebuilt their lives from scratch in Bombay, Thakurdas was simply trying to survive. He named his shop Sind Electric Stores, a quiet nod to the homeland he had left behind.

For four years, the shop held its own. Then, in 1968, Thakurdas died. He left behind five children. The eldest was Inder. Inder was fifteen years old.

Inder Thakurdas Jaisinghani did not go back to school. There was no time for that, and no money either. He stepped behind the counter of Sind Electric Stores and kept the lights on. Most fifteen-year-olds in that situation would have sold the shop within the year. Inder stayed for the next five decades — and transformed what he inherited into a business that would one day wire India's homes, factories, and infrastructure from one end of the country to the other.

The Pivot That Changed Everything

For most of the late 1960s and early 1970s, Inder and his brothers — Ajay and Ramesh — ran the hardware shop the same way their father had. They sold cables, wires, switches, and fittings to the contractors and electricians who came to Lohar Chawl every morning. Business was steady but thin. Margins on trading are always thin. You are selling someone else's product at someone else's terms.

Inder understood something early that most traders never do: the only way to escape the margin trap is to stop being a middleman. You have to make the thing, not just sell it. In 1975, after years of carefully saving every surplus rupee and studying how cables were made, the Jaisinghani brothers took the leap that would define everything that followed. They acquired a plot of land in the MIDC industrial estate in Andheri, Mumbai. On that plot, they built their first cable manufacturing plant.

It was a small factory. The machines were modest. The team was tiny. But the principle behind it was enormous: if you own the manufacturing, you own the price, you own the quality, and you own the future. That factory in Andheri was the seed of what would become Polycab India Limited, formally incorporated in 1983.

Thirty Years of Quiet Building

Polycab did not become dominant overnight. The company spent the 1980s and 1990s doing something unsexy and essential: making cables. Lots of them. Better than almost anyone else in India. While other companies chased glamour businesses, Polycab went deep into the manufacturing of power cables, control cables, instrumentation cables, and building wires. They invested in precision, quality certifications, and production capacity when their peers were focused on distribution.

By the early 2000s, Polycab had become the preferred supplier for some of India's biggest infrastructure projects. Power Grid Corporation, NTPC, Railways, state electricity boards — the clients read like a register of India's nation-building. No brand advertising. No celebrity endorsements. Just consistent delivery on specifications that left no room for error. When you are making cables for a power transmission line or a railway electrification project, one failure can be catastrophic. Polycab never gave anyone a reason to switch.

"We never chased the market. We built the capability first, and the market came to us."
— Inder T. Jaisinghani, Chairman & MD, Polycab India

By 2006, the company had expanded beyond wires and cables into the Fast Moving Electrical Goods (FMEG) category — fans, switches, lights, and solar products. It was a logical extension. If you are already in every electrician's van and every construction site's supply order, why not put your brand on every room in the building too? The FMEG bet took years to pay off, but it would eventually give Polycab a second growth engine when the core business matured.

The IPO That Stunned Dalal Street

When Polycab filed for its IPO in 2019, the financial press barely noticed. This was not a technology company with a flashy founder and a viral growth story. This was a cables company. Founded by a man who had dropped out of school. Run out of factories in Gujarat and Maharashtra. Selling products that go inside walls and under floors, invisible the moment the building is complete.

The market disagreed with the press. Polycab's IPO was subscribed 51.77 times over. Every major institutional investor wanted a piece. The reason, when you dug into the numbers, was obvious in retrospect: here was a company that had been India's largest cable manufacturer for years and almost nobody in the investor community knew its name. The stock listed at a premium and never looked back.

The Machine That Never Stopped

What makes Polycab genuinely hard to compete with is not any single product or patent. It is the breadth and depth of the manufacturing infrastructure the company has assembled over fifty years. Today Polycab operates 25 manufacturing facilities across India — in Gujarat's Halol, Daman, Silvassa, Nashik, Roorkee, and elsewhere. The company produces over 10,600 SKUs covering every type of wire and cable used in any application from a domestic house to a critical infrastructure project. The copper rod plant in Halol, a greenfield investment exceeding ₹1,100 crore, gives Polycab backward integration into its most important raw material.

The FMEG segment, once the poor cousin of the wires business, has grown into a credible second pillar. Polycab fans, switches, and LED lights now compete with Havells and Anchor across price points. The distribution network built over decades for wires — already inside every electrician's contact book — became the natural channel for FMEG products without needing to build it from scratch. This is the compounding advantage of patience: infrastructure you built for one purpose turns out to work perfectly for the next.

India's infrastructure supercycle is still in its early chapters. The government's thrust on housing for all, renewable energy transmission, railways electrification, and the semiconductor and electronics manufacturing push — every one of these programmes lands an order in Polycab's book. The boy who dropped out of school to sell wires built the company that now wires India's future.

The Lesson

The most powerful businesses are built on necessity, not ambition.

Inder Jaisinghani did not set out to build India's largest electrical company. He set out to keep his family alive. The ambition came later — born from the discipline that necessity demands. The lesson Polycab teaches is not about vision statements or growth strategies. It is simpler and harder: show up every day, make the thing better than anyone else, reinvest everything, and never stop. Fifty years of that, compounded, is what an empire looks like.
