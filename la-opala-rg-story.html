For Decades, India Imported Its Dinner Sets from China. One Kolkata Family Decided to Fix That.

The Beginning

The Gap That Nobody Wanted to Fill

Walk into any middle-class Indian home in the early 1990s and open the kitchen cabinet. What you found told a precise story about aspiration and its limits. The steel plates — functional, indestructible, entirely without elegance — served everyday meals. For weddings, festivals, and guests who mattered, families pulled out their “good” dinner sets: either imported European opalware that cost more than a month’s salary, or cheap Chinese opalware that chipped within a year and faded after three washes.

There was nothing in between. No Indian-made alternative that combined the durability of quality glass, the warmth of a white opal surface, and a price that a salaried household could justify. For Sushil Jhunjhunwala, a Kolkata industrialist with existing interests in glass manufacturing, this was not merely a market observation — it was an indictment. India was spending foreign exchange importing something it had every material capacity to make at home.

In 1988, he founded La Opala RG Ltd in Kolkata with a singular proposition: quality opalware made in India, priced for the Indian middle class. It would take years before the market caught up with him. But when it did, the company he built would be standing almost alone at the top.

A Kolkata industrialist with roots in glass manufacturing, Jhunjhunwala had the unusual combination of technical knowledge and market instinct to spot what India’s growing middle class actually needed. He didn’t enter opalware to chase a trend — he entered to correct an absence, building a factory-floor-first business that understood product before marketing, and quality before scale.

The Struggle

Fighting a Hundred Years of European Prestige — and Chinese Pricing — at the Same Time

Most founders fight one competitor. La Opala had to fight two entirely different enemies simultaneously, and both were formidable in ways that money alone could not solve.

On the premium end stood European opalware brands — Corelle, Luminarc, and others — that had been building their reputations since the late 19th century. For the Indian aspirant class, these brands carried the unspoken endorsement of a century of European kitchens. Price was almost irrelevant; the prestige was the point. Convincing a household that an Indian brand deserved the same status as these heritage names was a project in cultural persuasion, not just product quality.

On the value end sat Chinese opalware — flooding Indian markets through the 1990s at prices La Opala could not match without destroying margins. Chinese producers had scale advantages built over decades of export-first manufacturing, and they were willing to sell thin. Every rupee La Opala spent on better fluoride compound formulations, better firing temperatures, and better finishing was a rupee the Chinese competition was saving.

Adding to the complexity was distribution. Modern retail — the large-format stores that would eventually become La Opala’s most powerful channel — barely existed. The housewares and gifting market ran through a fragmented network of kirana-style gift shops, local crockery dealers, and wedding market vendors. Building a brand through that system, without a national advertising budget, required patient channel-by-channel persuasion rather than a single campaign.

The Chemistry Behind Your Dinner Set

Opalware gets its signature white colour and opacity from fluoride compounds added during glass manufacturing — the same class of chemicals used in toothpaste to protect enamel. The science that makes your plate brilliantly white is chemically related to the science that keeps your teeth healthy. La Opala’s mastery of this formulation is what separates premium opalware from the opaque-white imitation products that chalk and crack within months.

The Turning Point

When a Product Line Changed the Whole Story

The shift came not from a cost-cutting exercise or a sales campaign but from a decision about what kind of company La Opala wanted to be. Sometime around 2010, the company launched the Diva product line — a premium opalware range that positioned itself not merely as a domestic alternative to imports, but as a design-forward product in its own right. Diva was not trying to beat European brands on price. It was trying to make European brands irrelevant by being something Indian consumers could call their own with genuine pride.

The timing aligned with a structural change in Indian retail. Large-format stores — Shoppers Stop, Lifestyle, Croma, and the expanding supermarket chains — were giving housewares a level of shelf presentation that gift shops never could. A premium product with strong visual identity could now be discovered, evaluated, and aspired to by middle-class shoppers who previously had no exposure to it. La Opala moved its emphasis from gift shops to these organised retail formats, and the channel shift amplified the brand repositioning.

From that moment, La Opala was no longer competing on price with Chinese opalware. It had established a positioning — quality Indian design at accessible premium prices — that Chinese imports could not credibly occupy. And it had left behind the value-segment fight that was slowly eroding margins and brand perception alike. The company had, in effect, invented a new market category rather than continuing to fight in the old one.

“We did not want to be the cheapest dinner set on the shelf. We wanted to be the one that made a household feel they had arrived — at a price that did not require them to wait until they had.”

— La Opala Management, on the Diva brand philosophy

La Opala’s customer is, at core, the aspirational Indian household — the couple in their thirties furnishing their first real home, the family upgrading from steel for a daughter’s wedding, the corporate gifting manager looking for something that says quality without saying foreign. This is a customer that India’s economic story keeps producing at scale, and that La Opala has been serving for over three decades.

Gifting has always been a particularly powerful channel. In India’s culture of occasion-based gifting — weddings, housewarming ceremonies, festivals, retirements — a dinner set occupies a very specific emotional register. It is aspirational without being ostentatious, practical without being mundane. La Opala’s Diva line, packaged in premium boxes with careful presentation, became one of the default answers to the question every gift-giver faces: something nice, something useful, something that lasts.

Why It Matters Now

India Is Premiumising — and La Opala Has Already Built the Factory

There is a tide running through India’s consumer economy right now, and La Opala is sitting squarely in its path. As household incomes rise — not in the top decile, but in the vast middle — hundreds of millions of Indians are making their first discretionary upgrades. Steel plates to opalware. Plastic containers to glass. Functional kitchenware to something with a design sensibility. La Opala built its brand in exactly this segment, at exactly this price point, over exactly the years when this consumer was beginning to exist at scale.

The government tailwind reinforces the opportunity. India’s push to reduce consumer goods imports — whether through tariff policy or the deeper cultural shift toward domestic brands — lands directly in La Opala’s territory. A product that was once chosen partly because it was imported is now chosen partly because it is Indian. The brand’s positioning as quality Indian design, rather than poor man’s European imitation, means it can ride both the premiumisation wave and the Make in India sentiment simultaneously.

Competitors exist — Borosil in borosilicate glass, Cello in the value plastic and glass segment — but none has La Opala’s specific combination of organised opalware dominance, gifting-channel depth, and design-led premium positioning. That is a narrow gap to have occupied for 37 years. It is also, as it turns out, a very well-defended one.

“Import substitution is not a strategy that expires. Every generation of Indian consumers discovers it for the first time — and when they do, they want a product that was made for them, not one that was made for someone else and sold here as a consolation.”

— On La Opala’s enduring market positioning
The Lesson

The most durable businesses are not the ones that disrupted an industry dramatically — they are the ones that saw a gap between what existed and what people actually needed, and filled it with patient precision. Sushil Jhunjhunwala did not invent opalware. He simply decided that India’s middle class deserved a version of it that was made in India, designed for India, and priced within reach of the households that were quietly building this country from the inside out. The product was a dinner set. The insight was something much larger: that aspiration, when you manufacture for it honestly, compounds the same way wealth does. La Opala is, at its core, not a glassware company. It is proof that filling a gap with integrity — rather than filling it cheaply or filling it loudly — is the only kind of moat that holds across decades.
