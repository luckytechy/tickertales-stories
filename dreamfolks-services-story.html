The Business Model

An asset-light two-sided platform with a natural monopoly

Dreamfolks does not own a single lounge. It does not own an airport. It has no planes, no hospitality real estate, no physical assets of any significance. What it owns is the relationship — the technology layer that connects two sides of a marketplace that cannot function without it.

On one side: banks, credit card companies, and card networks (Mastercard, Visa, RuPay). On the other: airport lounges, spa operators, F&B outlets, and meet-and-greet services at airports across India and around the world.

Every time a transaction happens — a cardholder uses a benefit — Dreamfolks earns a fee. The platform processes the eligibility check, the authorisation, and the settlement. No phone calls. No paper registers. Just a real-time API that handles the entire flow in under a second.

The genius of the model is that switching costs are enormous on both sides. A bank that has integrated Dreamfolks into its card benefit stack would need months of engineering work to replicate that integration with a competitor — assuming a competitor of equal capability even existed. On the lounge side, a lounge operator that runs Dreamfolks terminals is not going to rip them out. The platform has no meaningful domestic competition at scale.
