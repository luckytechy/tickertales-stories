An Unusual Origin

Born from Displacement, Built for the Excluded

Repco Home Finance’s story begins not in a boardroom but in the aftermath of ethnic violence. Repco Bank — the parent company — was founded as the Repatriates Co-operative Bank, specifically to serve Indian repatriates expelled from Sri Lanka and Myanmar during the ethnic tensions of the 1970s and 1980s. These were Indian-origin people who had lived abroad for generations and were forced to return to India with almost nothing. No assets. No documentation. No credit history. No mainstream bank would touch them.

Repco Bank was created to serve exactly those people — the ones the system had excluded. That founding philosophy — that creditworthiness is not always captured by documents — ran deep in the institution’s culture. When Repco Home Finance was spun off in 2000, it inherited that same DNA. And it found an even larger version of the same excluded population waiting for it.

The Insight

80% of India Earns Without a Salary Slip — and Every Bank Turns Them Away

When Repco Home Finance launched in 2000, the home loan market in India was built for one type of borrower: the salaried employee. Form 16, salary slips, bank statements — the entire underwriting process assumed you worked for an employer who deducted TDS and deposited your salary into an account. If you were a carpenter, a vegetable vendor, a small shop owner, an auto driver, a kirana operator — you earned well, possibly more than a mid-level salaried employee. But you earned in cash. Every bank, every NBFC, every housing finance company sent you away.

Repco built its business around those borrowers. Not as charity — as deliberate strategy. The founders understood that if you could accurately assess the income of a self-employed borrower using field-based observation rather than paper documentation, you had access to an enormous, underserved market with very little competition.

The Method

When You Cannot Read the Documents, Read the Business

Repco’s credit assessment model is built on what the company calls field underwriting — a process that sounds simple but requires significant institutional knowledge to execute consistently. Instead of asking for salary slips, Repco’s field officers visit the borrower’s place of business. They look at inventory levels, assess customer footfall, speak to neighbouring shopkeepers, and observe the physical evidence of business activity. They build an income estimate from observation, not from paper.

“The borrower with a salary slip is easy to underwrite. The carpenter who earns ₹50,000 a month in cash is not — but refusing to lend to him is not prudence. It is ignorance dressed up as caution.”

The Lesson

Every mainstream institution saw a risk when a carpenter without a salary slip asked for a home loan. Repco saw a market. The difference was not recklessness — it was method. By developing field-based credit assessment rooted in real observation of real businesses, Repco built an underwriting capability that no paper-first lender could match. The origin of this capability traces back to a cooperative bank founded for displaced repatriates — people the system had failed first. Sometimes, the businesses that learn to serve the excluded become the ones who cannot be replaced.
