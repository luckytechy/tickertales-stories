Chapter One

The Business Hiding Inside Every Bottle, Packet, and Strip

Shiva Kabra

Founder, Control Print Ltd

A Mumbai entrepreneur who spotted a blind spot in Indian manufacturing — the machines that stamp dates on products — and spent three decades building a dominant domestic alternative to the global giants that had taken the market for granted.

Pick up any product you bought today — a medicine strip, a biscuit packet, a milk carton, a bottle of cooking oil. Somewhere on it, a date is printed. A manufacturing date, an expiry date, a batch number. You've seen it a thousand times without ever wondering: how did that date get there?

It got there at a factory, printed by a machine running at up to 80,000 decisions per second — an industrial coding and marking machine. It is a piece of mission-critical equipment. If it breaks down, the production line stops. And until the 1990s, almost every such machine in India came from abroad: Domino from the UK, Videojet from the USA, Markem-Imaje from France.

In 1991, Shiva Kabra looked at this market and saw something the multinationals had missed: a massive, growing domestic opportunity in a segment they were pricing too expensively for India's mid-tier manufacturers. He incorporated Control Print in Mumbai and started building the machines himself — competing head-to-head with companies backed by billion-dollar global parents.

⚡ Tech Trivia

The continuous inkjet technology inside these machines ejects ink from a nozzle at up to 80,000 drops per second, electrically deflecting each drop to form characters — the same basic physics as the first inkjet printers of the 1970s, now running at production-line speeds on a factory floor.

Chapter Two

The Razor-and-Blade Insight That Changed Everything

Here is how Gillette made its fortune: sell the razor cheap, sell the blades expensively, forever. It is one of the oldest and most durable business models in existence. Shiva Kabra brought this logic to the industrial equipment world.

Control Print's machines are sold at competitive prices — sometimes aggressively so. The real money comes from the inks, solvents, and laser consumables that each installed machine needs to keep running. These are high-margin, recurring, and almost impossible to switch away from without shutting down your production line.

This matters enormously. A factory that installs a coding machine on a live production line doesn't casually swap it out. Switching means line stoppage, operator retraining, re-qualification, and downtime. So every machine Control Print installs is effectively a long-duration annuity — generating consumables revenue year after year, invisibly, reliably.

With over 60,000 machines installed across India, that is a very wide pipe of recurring revenue. And it grows every time a new machine is placed — at a biscuit factory in Ahmedabad, a pharma plant in Hyderabad, a dairy co-operative in Anand.

"The machine is the handshake. The ink is the marriage. And nobody gets divorced from a production line."

The Journey

From One Mumbai Office to 60,000 Machines Across India

1991
Control Print founded in Mumbai
Shiva Kabra incorporates Control Print Ltd in Mumbai, entering the industrial coding and marking machine market dominated by Domino, Videojet, and Markem-Imaje. His pitch: comparable quality at a price point that works for India's growing FMCG and pharma manufacturers.
Late 1990s
The razor-and-blade model takes root
Control Print begins building its installed base across FMCG and pharma sectors. With each machine placed, the recurring consumables business grows. The model is slow at first — but each installation is permanent, and the consumables revenue is predictable and compounding.
Early 2000s
Amul says yes — and everything changes
Winning Amul — India's most quality-obsessed dairy cooperative — as a reference customer is a turning point. When Amul trusts Control Print machines on its production lines, every FMCG procurement manager in India takes notice. The credibility gap between an Indian brand and the global incumbents begins to close.
2013
Listed on NSE and BSE
Control Print lists on both major Indian stock exchanges as CONTROLPR, giving public investors access to the razor-and-blade recurring revenue story embedded in India's manufacturing growth. The listing brings capital to accelerate R&D and geographic expansion.
2018–2022
Pharma serialization becomes a structural tailwind
Government mandates around pharmaceutical serialization and track-and-trace require every medicine pack to carry unique identifiers — creating a wave of mandatory upgrades to advanced coding equipment. Control Print is positioned perfectly for this structural demand cycle.
FY2025
60,000+ installed machines, ~₹430 Cr revenue
Three decades after Shiva Kabra started the company with a belief that India could make these machines domestically, Control Print has over 60,000 installed units running inside Indian factories — stamping dates on products that reach every home in the country.
Chapter Three

Why Global Giants Couldn't Simply Crush Them

Domino and Videojet are not small companies. They have global R&D budgets, deep distribution networks, and decades of brand trust with large multinationals. Competing against them should have been impossible for a Mumbai startup.

But Control Print found a strategic seam. India's mid-tier FMCG and pharma manufacturers — a category that barely registers in the global giants' market strategy — needed machines that were affordable enough to buy and serviced by technicians who could actually reach them quickly. A Videojet service engineer flying in from Singapore is not the answer for a biscuit factory in Nagpur.

Control Print built a deep local service network, trained engineers across India, and priced its machines competitively. Over time, this domestic infrastructure became its own competitive moat — service speed and local parts availability that the global players structurally cannot match at the same cost.

And now, with laser coding — a higher-margin technology that requires no consumables but commands premium pricing — Control Print is moving up the value chain, even as its legacy installed base continues to generate steady ink-and-solvent revenue below the surface.

The Takeaway

What Control Print Teaches Every Investor

🖨️
The best recurring revenue businesses are the ones where customers don't notice they're paying.

Control Print built ₹430 crore in revenue by selling machines that nobody at the factory thinks about — until they stop working. The date stamps appear automatically. The ink gets reordered automatically. The relationship renews automatically.

This is the invisible compounding that razor-and-blade models produce. The machine is a one-time sale. The consumable is a permanent subscription. And in thirty-four years, Shiva Kabra placed sixty thousand of those subscriptions inside India's factories.
