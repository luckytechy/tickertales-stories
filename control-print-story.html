The Invisible Industry

Before a Product Leaves the Factory, a Machine Stamps Its Story on the Pack

Think about the last product you picked up at a pharmacy, supermarket, or kirana store. Somewhere on the packaging is a date: manufacturing date, expiry date, batch number, MRP. You read it in a second and moved on. But that date was put there by an industrial coding machine working at speeds of up to 80,000 decisions per second — ejecting microscopic ink droplets, deflecting them electrically, printing characters on a moving production line with no human hand involved.

In 1991, Basant Kabra — a chemical engineer from Mumbai — looked at this industry and saw a problem. The machines doing this work across India’s FMCG, pharma, and food factories were all imported from global companies: Domino (UK), Videojet (USA), Markem-Imaje (France). No domestic company was supplying them. Kabra decided to change that, starting Control Print Limited with the conviction that Indian manufacturers deserved a local, service-responsive alternative.

Basant Kabra
Founder & CMD, Control Print Limited

A chemical engineer by training, Basant Kabra founded Control Print in 1991 with the insight that India’s entire FMCG and pharma manufacturing sector depended on imported coding machines for a function as basic as printing dates. He built Control Print on service proximity — the idea that a domestic company could respond to a production line emergency faster than any global supplier. Over four decades, he turned that service advantage into 60,000+ installed machines. His son Shiva Kabra now leads marketing and strategy.

The Business Model

The Machine Is Almost Free. The Ink Is the Business.

Control Print’s genius is not the machine itself — it is the model underneath it. Like Gillette selling razors cheaply to capture the blade revenue, Control Print sells its coding machines at low or breakeven margins to get them onto production lines. Once installed, the machine needs inks, solvents, ribbons, and laser consumables — and those consumables generate recurring, high-margin revenue year after year.

The economics are almost self-reinforcing. A production line manager will not switch a coding machine mid-run. The requalification process — recalibrating the machine, retraining operators, testing on the production line — takes weeks and creates real risk of line downtime. The installed machine is essentially permanent until it physically fails. This gives Control Print sticky, predictable annuity-like income from every machine it has placed on a factory floor.

The Number That Locks In The Moat
Control Print has over 60,000 installed coding machines running inside Indian factories. Each one generates recurring consumables revenue every year — and switching costs make replacement almost unthinkable once installed on a live production line.

The turning point came when Amul — India’s most quality-obsessed dairy cooperative — chose Control Print machines for its production lines. That single reference customer changed everything. If Amul trusted Control Print, the credibility barrier with every other FMCG quality manager in India had effectively been broken.

The Lesson

Basant Kabra did not invent the coding machine. He did not discover a new technology. He found a niche — unglamorous, invisible, essential — that global companies dominated because no Indian entrepreneur had bothered to compete. He competed. He won Amul. He proved that a domestic company could meet the quality bar. And then he let the model do the rest: 60,000 machines, each one a small annuity, each one nearly impossible to remove once installed. The best businesses are sometimes built not on disruption but on relentless commitment to the unglamorous things that every factory must get done.
