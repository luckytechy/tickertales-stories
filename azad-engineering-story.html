The Founding

One Shed. One Machine. One Impossible Ambition.

In 2008, Rakesh Chopdar did something that looked, from the outside, like madness. He rented a 200 square meter shed in the Balanagar industrial area of Hyderabad. He bought a single second-hand CNC machine. And he decided he was going to manufacture components for jet engines — the most demanding precision machining work on earth.

Aerospace is not a forgiving industry. Jet engine components — turbine blades, aerofoils, nozzle guide vanes — operate at temperatures above 1,400 degrees Celsius. They spin at tens of thousands of RPM. A deviation of just a few microns can cascade into catastrophic failure. Global aerospace OEMs don't give second chances, and they don't give first chances to unknown Indian startups run by school dropouts from Hyderabad.

But Rakesh Chopdar had spent twelve years earning something that no college could have given him: the ability to machine metal to tolerances that aerospace demanded. And he was willing to prove it, one component at a time, without the luxury of brand name, industry network, or institutional backing.

The Science

What It Actually Means to Machine a Jet Engine Part

Most people have never thought about what holds a jet engine together. But consider this: a modern gas turbine engine contains hundreds of precision-machined components — turbine blades, vanes, discs, seals — that must withstand temperatures hotter than molten lava, while spinning at 10,000 RPM, for thousands of hours without failure.

The tolerance for error is measured in microns. One micron is one thousandth of a millimeter — roughly one-fifteenth the width of a human hair. Azad Engineering machines to these tolerances. Not approximately. Exactly.

The components they make go into jet engines powering commercial aircraft, military hardware, and massive gas turbines generating electricity for cities. A batch that fails quality inspection doesn't just get returned — it triggers audits, supplier reviews, and potentially the loss of a customer relationship that took five years to build.

This is why Rakesh Chopdar's achievement is so staggering. He learned this craft in his father's workshop, without an engineering degree, in a country where aerospace precision manufacturing was barely a cottage industry. Then he built a company that the world's most demanding OEMs trust with their most critical components.

The Bigger Picture

India's Aerospace Moment — And Why It Matters

India currently accounts for barely 1% of global aerospace exports. The global aerospace components market is worth hundreds of billions of dollars. The gap between those two numbers is the opportunity — and it is precisely the gap that Azad Engineering is positioned to close.

The company has a partnership with DRDO for turbo-gas generator engines. It signed a defence partnership with Rolls-Royce in 2024. It is building two new plants that will expand its capacity tenfold. With India's defence self-reliance push and global aerospace supply chains actively seeking China-plus-one alternatives, Azad sits at an extraordinary intersection of timing and capability.

Sachin Tendulkar — not known for casual investments — chose to back this company. That tells you something. So does the fact that Rolls-Royce, GE, Pratt & Whitney, and Honeywell all chose to source from a company in Balanagar, Hyderabad, when they could have sourced from anywhere on earth.

They chose Azad because precision is non-negotiable — and in precision, Rakesh Chopdar has spent a lifetime becoming non-negotiable.
