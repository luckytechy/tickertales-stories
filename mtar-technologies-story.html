The Beginning

A Quiet Bet in Hyderabad, 1970

In 1969, two mechanical engineers — P. Ravindra Reddy and K. Satyanarayana Reddy — made a decision that their colleagues thought was foolish. They were comfortable, secure employees in India’s public sector. They had pensions, government backing, and the implicit guarantee of the state. They walked away from all of it.

Their idea was radical for its time: that a small private company in Hyderabad could manufacture precision components for nuclear reactors and space rockets — products that India’s government had always assumed only state-owned entities could make. Not because private companies lacked the talent. Because nobody had ever tried.

They started MTAR in 1970 with a seed capital of roughly ₹3 lakh and set up a small machine shop in Balanagar, an industrial cluster on the outskirts of Hyderabad. Their first customer was not a commercial business. It was NPCIL — the Nuclear Power Corporation of India — and the contract was for coolant channel assemblies: the precision tubes that carry coolant through the core of a nuclear reactor. Get it wrong, and it is a catastrophe. There was no margin for error, and no playbook to follow.

P. Ravindra Reddy
Founder & Chairman, MTAR Technologies (1938–2016)

Born in Alluru village, Nellore district, Andhra Pradesh, Ravindra Reddy graduated in mechanical engineering and spent years in the public sector before making the leap into private precision manufacturing in 1969. He believed India’s most demanding manufacturing contracts did not have to be government monopolies. He spent the next four decades proving it — building MTAR into the most trusted private precision engineering company in India before passing away in January 2016.

The Moat Nobody Talks About

Trust Built Over Decades Cannot Be Replicated in Quarters

What MTAR built over fifty-five years is not just a factory. It is a trust relationship with India’s most demanding institutions — ISRO, NPCIL, and DRDO — each of which requires not just precise engineering but an entire quality culture: documented processes, zero-defect manufacturing, and the willingness to be audited at any time.

MTAR’s parts have flown on every GSLV and PSLV rocket that ISRO has launched. They manufacture the liquid propulsion systems at the heart of these rockets. A failure in these components is not a recall — it is a mission loss. This is not a relationship any institution gives to a new entrant. It took MTAR thirty years to earn it, and it cannot be reproduced in three.

“The moment you are ISRO’s trusted supplier for precision propulsion components, you are not just a vendor. You become infrastructure — as indispensable as the launchpad itself.”

— Observed by analysts covering MTAR’s 2021 IPO

Then came an extraordinary twist. In the early 2010s, MTAR began supplying hot boxes — precision fuel cell components — to Bloom Energy, a Silicon Valley clean energy company making solid oxide fuel cells. By FY2025, Bloom Energy accounted for roughly 62% of MTAR’s total revenue. A Hyderabad precision engineering company, built on ISRO contracts, had become a critical supplier to America’s clean energy transition.

The Lesson

There is a category of business that never gets written about in business magazines — not because it has failed, but because it has succeeded so completely at staying invisible. MTAR Technologies is that business. Two engineers left the comfort of government employment, chose the hardest possible customers, and spent fifty years earning trust that cannot be bought, marketed, or replicated. The rockets fly. The reactors run. Nobody mentions MTAR. That is, precisely, the point — and it is also the most durable competitive advantage a company can build.
