The Beginning

A Village, a Platform, and a Boy Who Refused to Stop

There is a railway platform somewhere near Coimbatore where a young boy sat for hours — not waiting for a train, but studying. Physics. Chemistry. Mathematics. The bus fare was too expensive. The train pass was a student concession. So every day, Arokiaswamy Velumani would arrive early, spread his books on the concrete floor, and study while the world moved around him.

His father owned no land. His mother sold milk and earned Rs 3 a day. When the time came to pay college fees, she pawned her bangles. That is the economy this story begins in — not one of privilege or connections or a head start, but of a family scraping hard enough to keep one boy in school.

Velumani was that boy. And he never forgot it.

The Making of a Scientist

Fourteen Years of Quiet Work at BARC

After his BSc, Velumani joined a pharmaceutical company in Coimbatore at a salary of Rs 150 a month. He sent most of it home. When that company shut down, he got a job as a lab assistant at the Bhabha Atomic Research Centre — BARC — in Mumbai. It was the kind of institution that gives a bright young man something rare: time to learn without the noise of commerce.

Over fourteen years, he worked his way up. He specialised in thyroid biochemistry — the science of how the thyroid gland regulates metabolism, growth, and nearly every function of the human body. He earned his PhD. He became a scientist. And somewhere in those years among centrifuges and sample vials, he saw something that troubled him: thyroid testing was expensive, inaccessible, and deeply fragmented.

A thyroid test in 1995 cost around Rs 600. For a country where millions of people — particularly women — suffered from undiagnosed thyroid disorders, that price was a wall. Velumani, who had grown up on the wrong side of many such walls, decided he would knock this one down.

"I had a secure government job, a pension, and a future anyone would envy. But I could see that the test price was the problem. If I could centralise the processing, I could cut it by 75%. I had to try."

The Leap

One Lakh Rupees, One Room, One Big Idea

In 1995, Velumani resigned from BARC. He took his provident fund — Rs 1 lakh — and set up a tiny thyroid testing lab in Navi Mumbai. No investors. No co-founder. No marketing budget. Just a scientist who understood thyroid biochemistry better than almost anyone in India, and a conviction that diagnostics could be made affordable.

The early years were brutal. The market didn't trust a lone scientist in Navi Mumbai. Doctors were used to sending samples to established labs. Revenue was negligible. There was no investor line at his door — the diagnostics industry didn't seem fundable, and a single-scientist operation in an unfashionable part of the city didn't inspire confidence.

Velumani's wife Sumathi ran the household on almost nothing while he built the lab. He has spoken about those years with unguarded emotion — not just the financial hardship, but the psychological weight of choosing uncertainty over security when you have nothing to fall back on.

The Turning Point

The Hub-and-Spoke Model That Changed Everything

The insight that transformed Thyrocare from a struggling single lab into India's diagnostics revolution was deceptively simple: centralise the processing, decentralise the collection.

Most diagnostic labs at the time operated independently — each franchise collected samples and processed them in-house. Equipment was expensive to maintain. Quality varied. Prices were high. Velumani's idea was to invert this entirely. Build one world-class central processing laboratory. Fly samples in from across the country. Process them at scale. Pass the cost savings on to the customer.

The hub-and-spoke model meant that a franchise in Kolkata, Coimbatore, or Kanpur didn't need a centrifuge. They just needed to collect the sample, package it correctly, and ship it to the Navi Mumbai hub. Thyrocare would handle the rest — and charge Rs 150 for a test that elsewhere cost Rs 600.

The economics were brutal in the right direction. Centralisation allowed Thyrocare to process enormous volumes at low marginal cost. Volume allowed further price cuts. Lower prices unlocked a market that barely existed before — the millions of middle-class Indians who knew they should get tested but couldn't justify the cost.

The Network

1,000 Centres. One Central Lab. A Revolution in Reach.

By the time Thyrocare went public in 2016, the company had built what might be India's most elegant B2B franchise model in healthcare. The central processing laboratory in Navi Mumbai became one of the highest-volume diagnostic facilities in Asia. Samples arrived daily from across India — packaged, labelled, and shipped by franchise partners who needed no medical processing equipment of their own.

This asset-light model meant Thyrocare could scale without proportionally scaling costs. Each new franchise added revenue without requiring Thyrocare to build another lab. The marginal cost of one more collection centre was negligible. The marginal revenue was real.

The Lesson

The most durable competitive advantages are sometimes disguised as beliefs. Velumani didn't just build a cheaper lab — he built a different philosophy about who diagnostics should serve. When your founding conviction is that healthcare belongs to everyone and not just those who can afford it, you make decisions that incumbent players won't. You cut prices when others raise them. You fly samples to one hub instead of building expensive local labs. You grow a franchise network while staying asset-light. The railway platform boy had studied enough to know that the real test in life — like the real test in diagnostics — is not who can afford to take it, but who has the courage to make it available to all.
