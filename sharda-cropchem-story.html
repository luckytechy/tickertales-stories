The Beginning

Mumbai, 1994. A Gap in Plain Sight

Walk into any farming cooperative in France or Germany in the early 1990s and you would find crop protection chemicals — fungicides, herbicides, insecticides — priced far above their underlying cost of production. The molecules were old, mostly off-patent for decades. The science was settled. Yet European farmers paid premium prices because the distribution chain between chemical manufacturers and the end buyer was fragmented, slow, and dominated by a handful of large Western agrochemical houses.

Ramprakash V. Bubna, surveying this landscape from Mumbai, saw something that most people in the Indian chemical industry missed entirely. The expensive part of the business was not making the molecule. The expensive and defensible part was the product registration. In Europe, in Australia, in Canada — before any crop protection chemical could be sold legally, it had to clear a gauntlet of safety, environmental, and efficacy testing that took years and cost millions per molecule per country. Most small players could not afford it. Most large Indian manufacturers had no interest in it. The registration document, not the factory, was the castle wall.

Bubna founded Sharda Cropchem in 1994 with a deceptively simple thesis: source off-patent agrochemical formulations from manufacturers in India and China, invest systematically in building product registrations across as many countries as possible, and then distribute through those registrations to buyers who had no other option. No manufacturing capex. No research and development overhead. Pure focus on the bureaucratic moat that everyone else found too tedious to build.

The Mental Model

Pharma Generics, But for Crops

To understand what Sharda Cropchem built, it helps to think about the Indian generic pharmaceuticals industry. Companies like Sun Pharma, Cipla, and Dr Reddy's do not invent new drug molecules. They wait for patents on existing molecules to expire, then manufacture and distribute those molecules globally — cheaper, faster, and in enormous volumes. The barrier to entry is not scientific genius. It is regulatory navigation: obtaining bioequivalence approvals, navigating the US FDA's ANDA process, building relationships with pharmacy chains and hospital procurement offices.

Sharda applied an identical mental model to crop protection chemicals, decades before anyone thought to frame it that way. Off-patent fungicide? Source it, register it in thirty countries, distribute it at a price that undercuts the incumbent Western supplier. The business logic is identical. The industry context is different enough that very few observers ever drew the parallel. Sharda's competitors remained fixated on molecules and factories while Bubna quietly assembled a registration portfolio that spanned continents.

The Hard Part

Years of Investment Before a Single Rupee Returns

The registration moat is real, but building it is genuinely painful. Each country has its own regulatory framework. Europe's system — governed by EU Regulation 1107/2009 — is among the most demanding in the world. A single molecule registration in the European Union requires independent toxicology studies, environmental fate assessments, ecotoxicology data, and residue trials across multiple crops and geographies. The dossier submitted to the relevant national competent authority runs to thousands of pages. The review process typically takes three to five years. The cost, per molecule per zone, runs into millions of euros.

Sharda had to fund all of this before a single kilogram of product could be sold. For years, the company's registration expenses were a cash drain with no visible payoff. There were no shortcuts. Regulatory approvals cannot be rushed, and they cannot be acquired from a competitor — they are issued to specific entities for specific formulations. A new entrant who decided in 2010 to replicate Sharda's EU portfolio would, at best, have those registrations around 2014 or 2015, having spent tens of millions in the process. By then, Sharda already had established distributor relationships, pricing history, and customer loyalty.

This is what made the business defensible in a way that factories simply are not. A competitor can build a factory in eighteen months. Nobody can replicate a decade of regulatory filings in eighteen months.

The Turning Point

When Europe Opened Every Other Door

The European registration portfolio, painstakingly assembled through the late 1990s and 2000s, became the keystone of everything that followed. For a crop protection distributor, EU approval functions as a global quality certificate. When Sharda approached distributors in Australia, Latin America, or Southeast Asia with a molecule already registered in Europe, the conversation changed. EU-registered meant EU-scrutinised — the toughest regulatory standard in the world. That implicit quality signal made distribution agreements easier, customer conversations shorter, and pricing conversations more favourable.

By the time Sharda listed on the NSE and BSE in 2014, the company had built a presence across more than 50 countries with an asset base that a traditional chemical manufacturer would struggle to recognise. There were no reactors on the balance sheet, no depreciation schedules for processing plants, no environmental liability from manufacturing operations. The assets were intangible — registrations, relationships, and a reputation built on consistent supply and quality. Return on capital was structurally high because the capital base was structurally lean.

Revenue grew from roughly ₹1,400 crore in FY19 to a peak of ₹2,900 crore in FY23, fuelled partly by post-pandemic agrochemical demand and partly by China+1 sourcing trends that directed procurement away from Chinese manufacturers toward Indian distributors. When the cycle turned and agrochemical prices corrected in FY24, revenue dipped to around ₹2,200 crore — but the business model itself was never in question. The registration portfolio held its value through the downturn and the company returned to growth in FY25.

The Global Footprint

Seventy Countries, Zero Factories

Sharda Cropchem's distribution network covers more than 70 countries across six continents. Europe is the anchor — not just because of the revenue it generates, but because of what EU registration signals to every other market. Latin America is a significant growth region, particularly Brazil and Argentina where soybean and corn cultivation drives enormous crop protection demand. Australia and New Zealand round out the Anglophone regulatory markets. Africa and Southeast Asia represent the next frontier.

The product mix covers the three main categories of crop protection: herbicides (weed control), fungicides (disease control), and insecticides (pest control). Herbicides tend to be the largest revenue contributor, with glyphosate and other broad-spectrum products serving vast acreage in Europe and the Americas. Each product in each country represents a separate registration filing — a separate moat brick. Sharda's portfolio of 100+ active registrations is the accumulation of three decades of bureaucratic persistence.

The Bigger Picture

Why the Registration Moat Gets Stronger From Here

Three macro forces are converging in Sharda's favour. First, China+1 sourcing strategies have accelerated after supply chain disruptions during 2020–2022. Buyers in Europe and Latin America who were sourcing agrochemical active ingredients exclusively from Chinese manufacturers are diversifying toward Indian suppliers. Sharda, as a well-registered distributor with an established compliance track record, is a natural beneficiary of that reorientation.

Second, the EU Green Deal is systematically phasing out certain older agrochemical chemistries deemed harmful to biodiversity. As these molecules are withdrawn, approved alternatives need to be registered and distributed at scale. Each transition creates a new registration opportunity — and Sharda has both the regulatory expertise and the financial capacity to file aggressively for emerging molecules while competitors are still digesting the old portfolio.

Third, climate change is altering pest and disease pressure in ways that increase crop protection demand globally. New fungal diseases, shifting insect ranges, novel weed species — all of these require new registrations and new product launches. The market for off-patent generics is not shrinking. It is growing, and the companies with the largest registration portfolios will capture the greatest share of that growth.

The Lesson

Ramprakash Bubna's story is a reminder that the most durable competitive advantages are often the ones that look least like advantages from the outside. A filing cabinet full of regulatory approvals does not inspire venture capital pitch decks. It does not make for dramatic founding mythology. But when those approvals span 70 countries and cover 100 molecules and represent three decades of patient, expensive, tedious work, they become something almost impossible to dislodge. The factory was never the point. The right to sell — validated by regulators in dozens of countries — was always the real asset. Every business has its equivalent: the thing that takes too long to build for most competitors to bother, and that pays quietly for decades to those patient enough to start early. Sharda found theirs in a registration dossier, and the world's farmers are the customers.
