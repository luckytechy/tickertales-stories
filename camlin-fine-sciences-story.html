The Origin

You Know the Brand. You Don’t Know the Business.

Ask any Indian who grew up in the 1980s or 1990s about Camlin, and you’ll get the same answer. The geometry box. The erasers. The crayons in a tin with the golden logo. Camlin was the brand of Indian childhood — dependable, familiar, everywhere.

But hidden inside the same Camlin conglomerate, almost invisible to the consumer world, was a division making something entirely different. Not pens. Not stationery. Food antioxidants. BHA, BHT, TBHQ — the molecules that keep packaged biscuits from turning rancid, extend the shelf life of cooking oils, and preserve the crunch in your potato chips. This division was serving global food manufacturers while generations of Indian children were drawing with Camlin pencils and never connecting the two.

In 2007, via a formal scheme of arrangement, that division was demerged and listed separately as Camlin Fine Sciences Ltd (CFS). What followed was one of the most understated global success stories in Indian specialty chemicals — a company that went from a domestic chemicals unit to an exporter reaching 90+ countries, competing directly against BASF and SI Group, and manufacturing an ingredient found inside billions of packaged food items consumed every day around the world.

The Business Nobody Talks About

Preserving the World’s Food, One Molecule at a Time

Most consumers have never heard of BHA, BHT or TBHQ. But walk into any supermarket in the world — in Germany, Brazil, the United States, or South Korea — and you will almost certainly encounter these molecules on an ingredient label. They are synthetic antioxidants, and their job is simple: stop fat from oxidising. Without them, edible oils turn rancid within days, biscuits go stale, and packaged snacks lose both flavour and safety.

Camlin Fine Sciences does not just make these antioxidants. It formulates them into blended antioxidant solutions — proprietary mixes tailored to specific food applications, temperatures and regulatory requirements across different countries. A blended solution for a bakery in Texas has a different composition from one for a frying oil manufacturer in Malaysia. This is not commodity chemistry. This is knowledge-intensive specialty work, and CFS spent decades building the expertise to do it at scale.

The 50% Global Market Share Secret

Camlin Fine Sciences is among the world’s largest integrated manufacturers of Diphenols — specifically Hydroquinone and Catechol — and holds up to a 50% global market share in food antioxidants TBHQ and BHA. These molecules keep the biscuits, chips, and cooking oils on every supermarket shelf from going rancid.

CFS operates across two distinct, high-value product lines that are often confused but are entirely different chemistries. The first is Diphenols — specifically Hydroquinone (HQ) and Catechol. These are the core chemical building blocks from which food antioxidants TBHQ and BHA are derived. Controlling Diphenol production means controlling the entire antioxidant supply chain from raw material to finished blend, and CFS is among the world’s largest integrated manufacturers in this category. The second is Diphenylamine (DPA) — a chemically unrelated compound used as a post-harvest treatment on apples in cold storage to prevent superficial scald, the browning of apple skin after months of refrigeration. CFS is a key global supplier of DPA as well. Two separate chemistries, two separate global markets — both dominated by one Indian company.

The single most important move in Camlin Fine Sciences’ history came in 2011. The company acquired Borregaard Italia SpA, a hydroquinone and catechol manufacturing facility in Ravenna, Italy. This was not just a capacity addition. It was a supply-chain masterstroke. CFS had been heavily dependent on Hydroquinone (HQ) as its primary raw material and was battling volatile prices and chronic shortages that squeezed margins and constrained growth. By acquiring the Ravenna plant — a large, automated HQ manufacturing facility — CFS secured its own captive supply of the critical raw material overnight. Suddenly, they were not a buyer at the mercy of HQ market swings. They were a vertically integrated manufacturer, with full cost control from raw molecule to finished blend.

This is how you build a durable global business in specialty chemicals. Not by outsourcing your raw material risk, but by owning it. The Ravenna acquisition transformed CFS from a formulator into a fully integrated global player — cost-competitive at scale in a way no Indian competitor could easily replicate.

The Competition

Going Up Against Goliaths — and Winning Market Share

Camlin Fine Sciences does not compete in a gentle market. Its primary competitors in food antioxidants are BASF — the German chemical giant with revenues of over €70 billion — and SI Group from the United States, a specialist chemicals company with decades of entrenched relationships in global food manufacturing. In Diphenols and food antioxidants, the competition includes established European producers and increasingly aggressive Chinese manufacturers willing to compete purely on price.

The Chinese threat is real and persistent. Chinese commodity antioxidant producers have driven down prices aggressively, and CFS has had to navigate this by moving up the value chain — away from pure commodity BHA/BHT and toward formulated blends with proprietary compositions, better technical service, and the ability to meet country-specific regulatory requirements that commodity Chinese suppliers often cannot. This is the classic specialty chemicals playbook: escape the commodity trap by becoming indispensable.
