The Beginning

One Truck, One Loan, and Absolutely No Way to Track It

In 1976, Vijay Sankeshwar was a young man from Gadag — a small town in northern Karnataka where his family ran a modest printing press. He had no logistics training, no business pedigree, and no experience running a transport company. What he had was a borrowed sum of ₹2 lakhs and a conviction: he would buy one truck and start moving cargo across Karnataka.

The world he operated in was nothing like today. There were no mobile phones, no GPS, no satellite tracking. When the truck left with a delivery, Vijay had no way to contact the driver or know if the cargo had reached its destination. He only found out when the truck physically returned to Gadag — sometimes days later. Every trip was an act of faith. Every safe return was a small relief.

He suffered accidents. Cargo was damaged. Customers complained. Most people in his position would have stopped. Vijay kept going — one truck, one route, one day at a time.

The Struggle

Building Trust When Nobody Knew Your Name

India's roads in the late 1970s were unforgiving — potholed, narrow, and poorly lit. Breakdowns were frequent. The absence of any real-time communication meant that a delayed delivery could go unexplained for days, and customers had no visibility into why their goods had not arrived. In this environment, building a reputation for reliability was the only thing that mattered.

Vijay understood this intuitively. He refused to cut corners on vehicle maintenance. He personally ensured drivers were well-treated. And he reinvested every rupee of margin back into more trucks — not into a bigger office or personal comfort. The discipline compounded quietly.

By 1978, he had moved to Hubballi and added three more trucks to the fleet. By 1983, the operation had grown enough to be formally incorporated as Vijayanand Roadlines Limited. By 1990, VRL had 117 vehicles and a turnover of over ₹4 crore — built entirely without any institutional funding or investor backing.

The Turning Point

The Moment a Trucker Became a Network Builder

The real shift in VRL's trajectory came when Vijay stopped thinking like a trucker and started thinking like a network builder. Individual trucks move cargo. But a structured hub-and-spoke network — with centralised sorting hubs, fixed routes, and coordinated timing — moves cargo reliably, predictably, and at scale.

This insight drove VRL's expansion through the 1990s and 2000s. As India's roads improved and the economy grew, VRL was already positioned with a physical network that competitors would need decades to replicate. The company steadily expanded beyond Karnataka into Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and eventually across India — always adding routes, always deepening coverage.

In 2015, VRL listed on the NSE and BSE — a milestone that put a public market value on nearly four decades of quiet, unglamorous building. By then, VRL had already built what no amount of capital could buy quickly: 2,000+ delivery points and a physical presence across every major city and hundreds of smaller towns in India.

The most durable businesses in logistics are not built on technology. They are built on networks — and networks take decades to build and cannot be replicated by a cheque.

The Business Model

Why the Network Is the Moat

VRL's competitive advantage is not technology. It is not capital. It is the physical density of a surface logistics network built over 48 years — routes connecting every major city to hundreds of tier-2 and tier-3 towns, operated by staff who know their routes the way Vijay Sankeshwar once knew his single truck's road from Gadag.

In logistics, the density of the delivery network is where money is made and lost. A company with 2,000+ delivery points and decades of local relationships cannot be easily replicated by a well-funded startup. Replication requires time — and in logistics, time is the one thing money cannot buy at pace.

GST formalisation post-2017 accelerated VRL's relative advantage further. As the logistics sector moved from an unorganised 80% market to a gradually formalising one, companies like VRL with proper compliance infrastructure and pan-India networks benefited disproportionately. The market was coming to VRL — VRL had simply been waiting for it for 40 years.

Why It Matters Now

GST continues to formalise the market, gradually eroding the price advantage of unorganised fly-by-night operators.

VRL sits precisely at the intersection of these trends. Its pan-India network, which took 48 years to build, becomes more valuable as demand for organised surface transport grows. Its reputation for reliability — forged in the pre-mobile era when there were literally no tools to verify a delivery had arrived — has only deepened over time.
