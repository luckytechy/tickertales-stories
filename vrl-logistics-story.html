The Beginning

Gadag, 1976: One Truck, No Signal, No Map

The year was 1976. Vijay Sankeshwar was a young man from Gadag — a small, unhurried town in North Karnataka, 450 kilometres from Bengaluru. His family ran a small printing press. What there was: a loan of ₹2 lakhs, and the conviction that moving goods from one place to another was a business worth building.

He bought one truck. And the first thing he discovered was a problem that would define the entire era: once the truck left Gadag on a delivery, he had absolutely no way to know where it was. No mobile phones. No radio. No GPS. The truck drove out, and then it was gone — until it physically returned to Gadag, sometimes days later. He would not know if the delivery had reached its destination until the truck came back.

Dr. Vijay Sankeshwar
Founder & Chairman, VRL Logistics Ltd

Born on 2 August 1950 in Gadag, Karnataka, Vijay Sankeshwar grew up in a family that ran a printing press — no connection to logistics, no industry background. He borrowed ₹2 lakhs in 1976, bought one truck, and started Vijayanand Roadlines Limited in Gadag. Over 48 years of grinding persistence through accidents, losses, terrible roads, and zero technology, he built India’s largest commercial fleet. Known in Karnataka as “Transportzar,” he was elected to the Lok Sabha in 2004 and again in 2014.

The Grind

No Technology. No Safety Net. Just the Next Truck.

The 1970s and 1980s were a brutal era for surface logistics in India. Roads were narrow, poorly maintained, and shared with cattle, bullock carts, and pedestrians. Diesel was rationed. Breakdowns were frequent. Cargo damage was common. And the regulatory environment — the freight tax inspectors at state borders, the paperwork required for inter-state movement — added hours and uncertainty to every delivery.

Sankeshwar absorbed all of it. He suffered road accidents that wrote off vehicles. He endured cargo losses that customers held him responsible for. He navigated financial setbacks that would have forced most people out of business. He kept buying trucks — two in 1977, three more in 1978, methodically adding capacity even when each addition was a risk he could barely afford.

The Fact That Captures The Era
In VRL’s earliest years, Vijay Sankeshwar had no way to communicate with his truck drivers once they left on a delivery. He learned each delivery had succeeded only when the truck physically returned to Gadag — sometimes days later.

By 1983 he had moved to Hubballi and formally incorporated Vijayanand Roadlines Limited. By 1990 he had 117 vehicles and a turnover of over ₹4 crore. The shift from running individual trucks to building a structured hub-and-spoke network was the turning point that separated VRL from the thousands of small trucking operators that never scaled.

The Moat

You Cannot Buy a Network. You Can Only Build One.

VRL’s competitive advantage is not technology, and it is not capital efficiency alone. It is network density — the physical reality of decades of route-building across South, West, and Central India. VRL’s hub-and-spoke infrastructure, with thousands of pick-up and delivery points, took forty-eight years to build. A competitor with more money could not replicate it in five years — because the bottleneck is not money. It is branch locations, local relationships, driver networks, and the institutional knowledge of which routes carry what volumes in which seasons.

The Lesson

Vijay Sankeshwar did not build VRL because conditions were good. He built it in conditions that were as difficult as Indian business gets — bad roads, no communication technology, financial setbacks, and an industry dominated by small operators who never thought about scale. He kept going anyway, one truck at a time, one route at a time, one decade at a time. The network he built cannot be bought. It can only be built the way he built it — slowly, persistently, with no dramatic shortcut. Sometimes the most enduring competitive advantage in the world is simply the willingness to show up for forty-eight years.
